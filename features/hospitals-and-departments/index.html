Hospitals & Departments

This is where your organisation’s structure is built. A hospital holds its
departments and its physical layout — floors, rooms and beds — which the
rest of MedOps uses for scheduling, admissions and billing.

Who manages this

Setting up hospitals, departments and the ward layout is done by the
Tenant Admin. Other roles can view this information, and nurses and
operations staff can update bed status.

Hospitals

A hospital record represents one facility in your organisation. When you
create or edit a hospital, you can set:

  • Hospital name and an optional branch name
  • Location — country, state, city and full address
  • Hospital type — Clinic, Multi-specialty, or Diagnostic
  • Departments offered — such as OPD, IPD, ICU, Emergency, Pharmacy,
    Laboratory and Radiology
  • Compliance details — GST identification number, clinical
    establishment licence, ABHA facility ID, and a drug licence number (needed
    when the hospital runs a pharmacy)

Each hospital is Active or Inactive. New hospitals start out active.

You can: create a hospital, edit its details, view it, and deactivate
it when it’s no longer in use.

Departments

Departments are the clinical divisions inside a hospital — for example
Cardiology, Orthopaedics, or the ICU. They can be added, renamed and removed
at any time as your hospital changes.

  • A department has a name and belongs to one hospital.
  • Department names must be unique within a hospital — you can’t have two
    departments with the same name in the same hospital.

You can: list a hospital’s departments, add a department, rename one,
and remove one you no longer need.

The ward layout: floors, rooms & beds

To admit patients, you first describe the physical space. MedOps uses a
simple hierarchy:

Floor → Room → Bed

A floor contains rooms, and a room contains beds. A patient
is admitted to a specific bed.

Floors

A floor is a level of the building (for example, “Ground Floor” or
“3rd Floor”). Each floor has a number and name, and can optionally be linked
to a department (such as an ICU floor).

Rooms

A room sits on a floor and is where patients stay. Each room has:

  • A room number (unique on its floor) and an optional name
  • A room type — General Ward, Semi-Private, Twin Sharing, Private
    Deluxe, Suite, ICU, CCU, or Emergency Room
  • Optional amenities (such as AC, TV, oxygen port or Wi-Fi) for the
    patient’s information

Beds

A bed is the individual place a patient is admitted to. Each bed has a
number and an automatically generated bed code that identifies it
exactly (its hospital, floor, room and bed). Every bed has a status:

Bed status Meaning
Vacant Ready for the next patient
Occupied A patient is currently admitted here
Under maintenance Temporarily out of use
Reserved for cleaning Being prepared after a discharge or transfer
Beds protect themselves

A bed that’s occupied can’t be edited or deleted, and its status can’t be
changed manually — it frees up automatically when the patient is discharged
or transferred.

Room pricing (tariffs)

Each room type can have a daily tariff so inpatient charges are
consistent. A tariff is made up of:

  • Base room rent per day
  • Nursing charge per day
  • Doctor (RMO) visit fee per day
  • Amenity surcharge per day

MedOps adds these up into a total daily charge for the room type, which
applies when a patient is admitted to a room of that type.
